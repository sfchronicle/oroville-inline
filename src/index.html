What happened at Lake Oroville?
By Emma O'Neill, John Blanchard, and Kurtis Alexander
<%= t.include("partials/_title_social.html") %>
A wet winter brought to light problems at the nation’s tallest dam, which controls water delivered across the state. As storms raged in early February, dam operators released heavy flows down the Feather River, but a crisis began when a crater opened on the main spillway. Days later, water poured over an emergency spillway – essentially a hillside – that had never been used. The erosion of the hillside prompted the evacuation of nearly 200,000 people downstream.
During the rainy season and the winter, rain water and snowmelt flow into Oroville from the mountains and then it flows out through the Feather River. During the past several years, there has not been a lot of rainfall nor snowmelt, so the system has been parched.

This year has not been like the last years, though. After five years of drought, California was socked by one of its wettest winters in decades. The small city of Oroville saw nearly twice as much precipitation this rain year, through February, as last.
Winter storms in the mountains around Oroville generated a lot of runoff, which poured into the state-run reservoir. The reservoir swelled to its highest level in years.

Damage to the main spillway forced dam managers to rely on other ways to release water from the lake. The other outlets, however, were problematic. The Hyatt Powerplant, which can normally release a small amount of water, was taken off line after the main spillway cracked. Chunks of concrete from the broken spillway were causing water to pool up near the power plant, threatening to damage the turbines. The lake’s emergency spillway also failed. On Feb. 11, when lake water reached a certain height, water began pouring over the lip of the emergency chute and onto a hillside, which began to erode instead of carrying the overflow safely to the river. A set of river outlets, which hadn’t been fully used in years because of an accident there, was also inoperable. Pooling water from the damaged spillway covered the outtake pipes.

While dam managers have been able to sufficiently release water from the lake, through the damaged main spillway, they worry that future runoff could test their success. Snowpack in the mountains is near record highs, and as it melts this spring, the lake’s inflow is likely to be large. Most reservoirs in California are similarly at or near capacity and are releasing large amounts of water downstream.
